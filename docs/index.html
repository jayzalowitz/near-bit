Under the hood

For the technically curious — here's what makes this possible.

Modified NEAR Protocol

Bitcoin Infinity is built on a fork of NEAR Protocol where Bitcoin addresses are first-class account identifiers. When you send coins to a Bitcoin address, the chain creates an account automatically.

RPC translation proxy

A lightweight Rust service sits between your wallet and the chain. It translates all 204 Bitcoin Core RPC commands into NEAR RPC calls in real time. Your wallet never knows the difference.

Native signature verification

The chain verifies Bitcoin-format secp256k1 signatures natively. When you sign a transaction with your Bitcoin key, the chain recovers your public key and verifies it matches your address.

Genesis UTXO import

A genesis builder tool reads Bitcoin's UTXO snapshot and converts every balance into a Bitcoin Infinity account. One satoshi equals 10^16 yoctoBIT, preserving full precision.

What happens to Satoshi's coins?

Roughly 1.1 million Bitcoin are believed to belong to Satoshi Nakamoto. On Bitcoin today, they just sit there doing nothing. On Bitcoin Infinity, they secure the entire network — and fund its future.

🔒

Permanently locked, never lost

Satoshi's original balances are frozen at their exact genesis amount. They can never be spent, transferred, or moved to any regular address. The coins are preserved on-chain, not abandoned.

⚗

Put to work securing the network

Instead of sitting idle, these coins are staked — actively validating transactions and securing Bitcoin Infinity through Proof of Stake. Over a million BTC worth of security, working for everyone.

🏦

Rewards fund the Bitcoin Infinity Foundation

Staking earns rewards. Since the original balance can't be touched, any excess above the genesis amount goes to a foundation that funds ongoing development, audits, and ecosystem growth. Satoshi's coins become the endowment for Bitcoin's future.

🔓

If Satoshi comes back, they get their coins

There's a built-in unlock mechanism. If Satoshi (or anyone holding the original private keys) signs a cryptographic challenge that could only be produced after Bitcoin Infinity launched, the coins are unlocked after a 7-day public waiting period. Until then, they work for the network.

Why this matters

On Bitcoin today, Satoshi's coins are a black hole — over 5% of the total supply sitting untouched since 2009. On Bitcoin Infinity, that same wealth actively protects the network and funds its development. No coins are destroyed, no rules are broken. If the owner returns, they get everything back. If they don't, the network and its community benefit instead of no one.

Quantum-resistant by design

Bitcoin's cryptography won't last forever. Quantum computers will eventually break the math that protects every Bitcoin wallet. Bitcoin Infinity has a plan — Bitcoin doesn't.

☢

The quantum threat is real

Bitcoin uses secp256k1 elliptic curve cryptography. A sufficiently powerful quantum computer running Shor's algorithm can reverse it — recovering private keys from public keys. Every Bitcoin address that has ever spent a transaction is at risk. It's not a question of if, but when.

🛡

Optional quantum-safe keys today

Bitcoin Infinity lets you register a quantum-resistant secondary key alongside your existing Bitcoin key. You don't have to use it now, but it's there when you need it. Supported algorithms include Dilithium, Falcon, and SPHINCS — all NIST-approved post-quantum standards.

🚨

Emergency activation when the time comes

When quantum computers become a real threat, validators can activate a "Quantum Bit" via supermajority vote. This triggers a 30-day grace period for everyone to migrate to quantum-safe keys. After the grace period, accounts without quantum protection are frozen until they upgrade.

🚀

Bitcoin can't do this

Bitcoin has no upgrade path for quantum resistance. Changing Bitcoin's signature scheme would require a hard fork that the network has never successfully executed for a change this fundamental. Bitcoin Infinity builds the migration path in from day one, so when the threat arrives, the solution is already deployed.
